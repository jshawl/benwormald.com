Memo
September 19, 2014

The New Republic Digital Strategy Memo John Updike is very excited about this.

2Tweet
By Benjamin Wormald Photo: Stan Grossfeld/The Boston Globe via Getty Images

First, I would like to thank Amanda Silverman for reaching out to me to discuss The New Republic’s future in digital media and your web production position. I’ve long been an admirer of the magazine and am excited at the prospect of working with you all.

Having explored newrepublic.com over the past week, I can gladly say that much of the heavy lifting for expanding your digital operation has already been completed. One of the most important aspects of good web production is ensuring that your content remains front-and-center, and the recent redesign of newrepublic.com does so in a way that is undisctracting, free of clutter, and readable on all devices.

That said, there are a number of ways that The New Republic’s web production could be made more efficient, its content be better conformed to best web practices and search engine optimization, and its print design more integrated with its web design. Among these practices include:

1. Designing a defined workflow

In speaking with Amanda, I got the sense that the current workflow for web content production is a bit anarchic, at least in part because there is not a dedicated web producer to coordinate different roles. A well defined workflow created with digital content in mind would go a long way to organize the process from idea conception and graphic designing to dissemination and analytics review. It would also work to establish a chain of accountability for projects that require collaboration between multiple parties.

2. Increasing reader engagement on-site

Another crucial element of good web design and production is keeping the reader engaged with your content and encouraging them to spend more time on your site. Having excellent content is the first step to achieving these ends, but there are many other ways to keep web readers interested. Amanda noted that the “How the Democrats Can Avoid Going Down This November” piece was something that she felt was an excellent piece, but didn’t gain much traction on the web, and indeed, it is a good place to start thinking about increasing reader engagement.

The text itself is informative and important, but unfortunately it looks as if the print version of the story was just reproduced on the web, which makes the piece visually unstimulating. Graphics play a key role in reader engagement. In “How the Democrats Can Avoid Going Down This November,” there are nearly 1,500 words before the reader encounters the first graphic about different kinds of voters. That is a lot of text to digest. But if the graphic is instead introduced after paragraph 3, when the concept of two kinds of voters is introduced, the reader is afforded a mental break and is provided a visual that they will be able to reference and better understand the content to come. This QED article does a much better job of establishing a visual aid early in the piece to supplement the text to come.

For longer feature articles, in-text animations can also be deployed to engage the reader and progress the narrative. The New York Times’s “Snowfall” has been lauded ad infinitum (at least in my circles), but other outlets like Pitchfork, ESPN, and Pew Research have intelligently utilized the long-form interactive essay to great success. I wouldn’t recommend this for every 500-word blog post on tnr.com, but it would be very effective for translating the design elements of features like “How the Democrats Can Avoid Going Down This November” from their beautifully packaged print editions into something that is more visually stimulating than a block of text on a web page.

On a smaller scale, ESPN’s “More Than A Shirt” is a great example of how The New Republic could make articles like “The Democrats' Best Senate Hopes: An Unorthodox Ranking” more engaging for the reader. ESPN’s content is fairly straightforward - a ranking of football clubs’ jersey endorsement deals - but adding a subtle parallax scrolling animation makes the (otherwise dull) page memorable. “The Democrats’ Best Senate Hopes” has well designed graphics, adding a dynamic presentation that makes the reader interact with the page both visually and tactilely would add subtle depth to what is otherwise another list article.

3. Better User Navigation

Paramount to the user’s experience on a site is clear, logical navigation. The user should always know where they are, how they got there, and how they can find similar content. This is particularly important in long-form articles like “How the Democrats Can Avoid Going Down This November.” Once the reader is deep into the body of the text, they don’t have any way to navigate the site without laboriously scrolling back to the top of the page. But by installing a responsive header (as I have crudely done on this page, for example), you can keep the the primary navigation in close proximity to the reader when they want it, but keep the focus on the content when they don’t. This retracting header is even more valuable on mobile devices where space is at a premium.

Creating a logical navigation is also essential for packages like the “New Science of Democratic Survival” bundle. While the links for the rest of the package on each article’s right rail is helpful, the other pieces seem more like related links than part of one package.

In contrast, Pew Research’s “LGBT in Changing Times” has a simple, dedicated homepage that includes all the pieces of the package in a way that is both easy to navigate and provides an aerial view of collection as a whole.

4. Creating a better taxonomy

Another simple yet crucial element of enhancing web navigation is the curation of a well defined, robust taxonomy structure. Many times while browsing the site, I found myself looking for more articles on a subject that interested me, but was not directed to a repository of all the content i wanted, either because of redundant or esoteric tagging. For example:

ISIS and ISIL

Technology and Tech

This Week in Sketches and ???

As you can see, a lot of valuable content is lost because of ineffective tagging. While it is far from glamorous, having a carefully created and maintained taxonomy can help ensure that the reader finds the content they are looking for and can pay serious dividends in SEO.

5. Thoughtful metadata for better SEO

As mentioned above, creating a proper taxonomy can go a long way to strengthening your site’s clout on search engines. Another way to strengthen your SEO is think carefully about how articles’ titles and descriptions appear on search engines.

The New Republic generally does this very well; searching Google for “democratic midterms 2014” yields a result on the first page. But there is always room for improvement:

While such an oversight may seem minor, these description fields play an important role in SEO. Striving to perfect metadata is key to obtaining new readers via search.

6. Revamped dissemination

The New Republic’s Twitter and Facebook pages are already successful at luring new readers by including images, using hashtags where appropriate, and creating eye-catching tweets and posts (how am I not going to click on “The Duck Penis Paradox: Internet pop science is democratizing the field and completely ruining it”?).

One area that is overlooked, however, is the much maligned newsletter. While it’s fallen out of favor because it is not very “social,” a well edited newsletter is still an excellent tool for disseminating content and attracting readers to the site.

Unfortunately, the TNR newsletter looks a lot like an advertisement:

In its current state, the mailing doesn’t really make the recipient want to visit the site so much as compel them to send it to the trash. But if the newsletter were reimagined as less of an afterthought and more of a weekly round-up of content TNR had published in the past week, replete with article teasers like those already being produced on Facebook, TNR could easily increase page views by tens of thousands.

This, of course, is by no means a definitive checklist of ways to enhance the production of digital content, but I believe these suggestions would improve the digital product in a way that is complimentary to the excellent content that exists on newrepublic.com. Some of these suggestions are simple best practices that are easy to fix, others will require a great deal of collaboration and careful planning to execute correctly. But they all have the same end in mind: the creation of a website that can showcase the magazine’s rich history of top-notch journalism and excellent print design and bring it to a wider digital audience. It is my hope that I can bring my ideas and experience to The New Republic to help it achieve that goal.

Benjamin Wormald is a web producer at the Pew Research Center.
